# Messaging-Document Discipline

Writing rules for brand and messaging documents — generalizable across all products. These
are the verification and craft rules that run before any strategy or messaging draft ships.
They are operating rules, not aspirations: every one is imperative and every one is auditable.

Source: Every Agent messaging framework session, July 13, 2026 (product referred to as
"@Every" / "the Every Agent," formerly Plus One). Companion files: line-level rules in
`references/copywriting-line-rules.md`, the failure ledger in `references/failure-ledger.md`.
For the research-side methodology these rules point at, see
`marketing-science/research/references/competitive-audits.md` and
`marketing-science/research/references/synthesis-methodology.md` (cross-referenced, not
duplicated).

## The rules

- **THE FOUR-BUCKET PROTOCOL (operating rule; runs before delivery, every draft).** Every declarative sentence in strategy or messaging content is classified before shipping: VERIFIED (source in hand, cited per rule 13) · DOC (from canonical strategy documents, named) · [H] (hypothesis, tagged inline with its validation step) · CUT (unsourceable — dies before delivery). There is no fifth bucket; "it sounds right" maps to CUT. This applies with equal force to the claims that don't look like claims: sales motions and buying journeys ("reached through the Operator," "installs bottom-up"), persona psychology, channel assumptions, competitive characterizations, and quote selection. These are where invention hides, because they arrive dressed as strategy or structure and nobody asks strategy for a citation. Audit mechanism: any sentence, asked "which bucket?", must have an instant answer.

- **RESEARCH BEFORE DRAFTING, ALWAYS.** Never write a need state, pain point, market claim, or persona detail and then look for support. Pull the evidence first, write from it second. If a claim category can't be evidenced, it gets written as a tagged hypothesis — never as a finding.

- **PROOF TRAVELS WITH THE CLAIM.** Every pain point or market claim carries its named study inline, in the same sentence or the one adjacent: source, year, sample size where it matters. Stats verified earlier in a project move with the claim into every downstream section.

- **HYPOTHESIS TAGGING.** Motivation and psychology claims about buyers are hypotheses until discovery interviews or first-party product data confirm them. Tag them [H] inline, in the section itself, with the validation step named. A brand doc that reads like we know people we haven't studied is fiction with confident diction.

- **RESEARCH GAPS GET RESEARCHED, NOT ESCALATED.** If a section needs data (channel behavior, media consumption, buyer research), find published sources online before flagging anything internally. Only escalate for data that genuinely cannot exist publicly (our own funnel, beta usage, internal metrics). Never leave a section stubbed with "pending [colleague]" when public research could fill it.

- **NO SCAFFOLDING PROSE.** Kill any sentence that describes the document's own structure ("three buyers, one thread," "same story, different door"). If a line could open a LinkedIn carousel, cut it. Every sentence is a claim, a reason, an instruction to a writer, or a sample line.

- **CASH IN THE ARCHITECTURE.** Downstream sections never cite upstream structure by label ("Pillar 2 leads"). They restate the actual message and show the sample lines. Labels are navigation, not argument.

- **SAMPLE LINES ARE MANDATORY.** A messaging section without example copy is strategy, not messaging. Two or three lines per audience or moment, cascaded from existing hero and pillar language — never newly invented per section.

- **COMPETITORS APPEAR IN EVERY BUYER-FACING SECTION.** State what named competitors currently say to that audience, verbatim where fetched, with fetch dates. Absence claims ("nobody offers X") carry the fetch date and a launch-week re-check flag. An open lane is only claimable if the doc shows who isn't in it.

- **FRAMEWORKS ARE USED, NOT CITED.** Reeves, Bernbach, Sharp, Romaniuk appear only when the doc answers them for this product ("For @Every: ..."). Never as decoration or authority gestures.

- **ADMIT WEAKNESS INLINE.** Where a claim is thin, say so in the section itself ("The Power: the weakest leg, honestly"), not in an appendix. Candor where we're weak buys credibility where we're strong.

- **WHEN THE PRINCIPAL REWRITES A SECTION, EXTRACT THE METHOD, NOT THE FORMAT.** Diff for operations: what merged, what got cut, where proof landed, what tone shifted. Apply those operations to the next section before drafting it. Mimicking headers and length is not learning.

- **VERIFICATION LAUNDERING CHECK.** The verification bar applies to soft claims exactly as it applies to stats. Psychology, motivations, and channel assumptions are where invented specificity hides, because nobody expects a citation after "what motivates them." Audit these hardest.

- **FULL CITATIONS, ALWAYS.** Every stat carries report name, publisher, year, and sample size on first use — "(Panopto)" is not a citation; "(Panopto Workplace Knowledge and Productivity Report, 2018, w/ YouGov, n≈1,000)" is. Also disclose verification depth (primary read vs. secondary coverage), sponsor conflicts (vendor surveys), and age risk (pre-2022 workplace data predates the remote/AI shift). Small samples get flagged inline (HBR's 1,200-toggles stat is n=137).

- **QUOTE THE COPY THAT PERSUADES, NOT THE COPY THAT INSTRUCTS.** A competitor verbatim in a messaging doc must be doing persuasive work on the buyer — a benefit claim, a promise, a positioning line. Setup instructions ("Grant Claude access to selected channels") are descriptors, not messaging, and quoting them as messaging fabricates a weak competitor. Pull from hero copy, announcement benefit language, and executive press quotes.

- **COMPETITIVE READS EXPIRE.** Any characterization of a competitor's positioning ("no human pain in their copy," "they don't message X") is dated to its fetch and dies when they ship. Before reusing a competitive read in a new section or draft, re-fetch. A true-three-weeks-ago claim shipped today is a fabrication with a paper trail. (Cadence complement: `marketing-science/research/references/competitive-audits.md` re-verifies audit claims older than ~4 weeks; this rule generalizes that to any competitive read before any reuse.)

- **WHEN A COMPETITOR CLAIMS OUR TERRITORY, SAY SO FIRST.** If a re-fetch shows a competitor now messaging a pain we planned to own, lead with that finding and move the wedge — never quote around it to preserve the existing differentiation. The doc's job is to be right, not to stay comfortable.

- **WHEN A CLAIM DIES, QUESTION THE FIGHT BEFORE PATCHING THE CLAIM.** A falsified differentiation claim is a signal to move up one level, not to search for a harder-to-falsify version of the same claim. Run the ladder explicitly: feature claims (fastest to copy) → mechanism claims (months to copy) → segment claims (require abandoning a position to copy) → identity claims (cannot be copied, only lied about). Every's strongest positions are identity-level — a fast-scaling small team selling to fast-scaling small teams, dogfooding as kinship proof rather than capability proof. Before shipping any differentiation, ask: could a funded competitor match this in a quarter? If yes, climb the ladder.
