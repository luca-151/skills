# Cadence — Feature Launch

The timing and sequencing framework for a feature launch. Mid-tier: more extended than
improvement, shorter than new-product. A feature launch has a real pre-launch window,
a coordinated launch day, and a follow-through period where the feature has a chance to
demonstrate itself in use.

Lead time from decision to launch: four to six weeks is typical. Below three weeks,
asset quality and coordination suffer. Above eight weeks, a feature announcement risks
being scooped by a competitor or overtaken by the next internal priority.

---

## Pre-launch sequencing

### T-30 (four weeks before launch)

- Launch narrative document written and locked. This is upstream of everything — no
  asset production begins until the narrative is done.
- Positioning confirmed. If the feature changes what the product is understood to do,
  the positioning skill for that product is loaded and the messaging architecture updated.
- Asset scope confirmed. Which channels are in scope for this feature launch? Not every
  feature warrants every channel — scope is a deliberate decision, not a default.
- Owners assigned for each channel asset.

### T-14 (two weeks before launch)

- All channel assets in first draft.
- Internal review of launch narrative and first draft assets — marketing lead, product
  owner, and at minimum one editorial reader.
- Product page update drafted and in review.
- If a teaser or warm-up moment is in plan, that content is drafted.

### T-7 (one week before launch)

- All assets final or in final revision.
- Product page update approved and staged, ready to go live on launch day.
- Distribution lists confirmed.
- Any teaser content scheduled (if applicable).
- Team briefed: anyone who might receive a user question knows the launch is coming,
  what the feature does, and what the announcement says.

### T-2 (two days before launch)

- All assets final. No substantive changes after this point.
- Scheduled sends confirmed.
- Product team confirms the feature is stable and ready.

### T-1 (day before launch)

- Final read of all assets.
- Confirm all scheduled sends and posts.
- Product team gives final green light on feature stability.
- Identify the single person responsible for launch-day monitoring and user response.

---

## Launch day

Feature launches have light choreography. The principle: lead with the channel that
reaches the most engaged existing audience, then fan out to acquisition channels.

- **Morning window**: email to current subscribers and paid users. This audience knows
  the product and will understand what the feature does — the most valuable early signal
  on how the announcement lands.
- **Same morning**: social posts across in-scope channels. Social extends reach beyond
  the current audience.
- **Within 24 hours**: product page goes live with updated feature content.
- Monitor user responses throughout the day. A new feature announcement can surface
  unexpected use cases, confusion, or bugs — route each to the right owner immediately.

---

## Post-launch follow-through

### T+1

- Check open rates, click rates, and social engagement.
- Review any user responses. Note patterns in what users are asking or saying.
- Product team checks for any stability issues triggered by increased usage.

### T+7

- If the feature is generating interesting user behavior or outcomes, identify whether
  there's a follow-up content moment — a user story, a data point, a practitioner take.
- Review whether the launch narrative held: did users understand what the feature does
  and why it matters, or did the announcement create confusion?

### T+14

- Brief retrospective: what worked, what would we do differently? What should carry
  forward into the next feature launch cadence?
- If user outcomes have emerged that warrant a follow-up piece (byline, thought-
  leadership, newsletter inclusion), hand off to the relevant craft skill.
