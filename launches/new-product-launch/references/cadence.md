# Cadence — New Product Launch

The timing and sequencing framework for a new-product launch. The heaviest tier — an
extended pre-launch window, a choreographed launch day, and a sustained post-launch
period during which the product proves its narrative with evidence from real use.

Lead time from positioning lock to launch: eight to twelve weeks is the floor. Below
eight weeks, asset quality and launch narrative depth suffer. The lead time is not
about asset production alone — it is about giving the product's narrative time to be
tested, refined, and made believable before it goes public.

The positioning and launch narrative must be locked before the lead-time clock starts.
If positioning is still in motion, the launch is not in pre-launch — it is still in
strategy.

---

## Pre-launch sequencing

### T-90 or earlier (strategy phase, before pre-launch begins)

- Positioning confirmed. The product's positioning skill is complete and reviewed.
- Launch narrative written, reviewed, and locked. This is the upstream document for
  everything that follows. See `references/launch-narrative.md`.
- Brand-voice confirmed. The brand-voice skill for this product is complete.
- Asset scope decided. Which channels, what sequencing, what formats — all decided
  before production begins.
- Press strategy decided: are we doing press? If so, who is the journalist relationship
  owner, what is the target outlet list, and what is the embargo strategy?

### T-60 (eight weeks before launch)

- Long-form assets (byline, thought-leadership) in first draft. These take the longest
  to develop and have the longest editorial review cycle.
- Landing page in first draft.
- Email drafts begun for all in-scope segments.
- Social asset suite outlined.
- Press outreach list finalized, if press is in plan.

### T-30 (four weeks before launch)

- Long-form assets in final revision.
- Landing page final or near-final.
- All email drafts complete.
- Social asset suite drafted.
- Press outreach materials (pitch, embargo release) drafted and reviewed.
- Internal team briefed — anyone at Every who interacts with external audiences should
  know the product is launching, what it does, and what the announcement says.
- If a waitlist, beta, or pre-launch signal mechanism is in plan, it should be live
  by now.

### T-14 (two weeks before launch)

- All assets final or in final revision.
- Landing page staged and ready for launch-day deployment (or already live in pre-launch
  form, if that's the strategy).
- Press outreach begins under embargo, if press is in plan.
- Distribution lists confirmed.
- Scheduled sends configured and confirmed.
- Product team confirms the product is stable and ready.

### T-7 (one week before launch)

- All assets final. No substantive changes after this point.
- Final internal alignment: every stakeholder who needs to know is briefed.
- Press embargo confirmed with all outlets, if applicable.
- Launch-day owner identified and briefed. One person is responsible for day-of
  monitoring and decision-making.
- Any pre-launch content (teasers, behind-the-scenes, waitlist communication) complete
  and scheduled.

### T-2 (two days before launch)

- Final asset read in context.
- Confirm all scheduled sends and posts.
- Product team gives final green light on stability.
- Press embargo dates and times confirmed.

### T-1 (day before launch)

- Final walk-through of launch-day choreography with the team.
- All systems confirmed: email, social accounts, landing page deployment, press embargo.
- The launch narrative is re-read by the launch-day owner. They should be able to
  describe the product in the narrative's terms without reference to the document.

## Launch day

A new-product launch has a choreographed sequence. The principle: lead with the audience
that will do the most with the news first, then expand outward.

Every's launch sequence (to be adapted based on product and context):

- **Morning, before 9am ET**: press embargo lifts, if press is in plan. Press coverage
  goes live before the Every-owned channels, so the external audience sees the product
  first in third-party context.
- **9–10am ET**: landing page goes live (if not already live in pre-launch form).
- **9–10am ET**: email to Every subscribers and current users. This is the announcement
  to the most engaged audience — the people who trust Every's editorial judgment and
  will respond to the launch as a signal of what Every is building.
- **Within two hours of email**: social posts across all in-scope channels.
- **Same day**: any long-form content goes live (byline, thought-leadership). These are
  the narrative-depth pieces that provide context beyond the announcement.
- **Launch-day owner monitors all channels** for user response, press pickup, and any
  issues requiring a real-time decision.

## Post-launch follow-through

### T+1 through T+3

- Review all channel engagement: email open and click rates, social engagement, press
  coverage volume and tone.
- Review user responses. Early user response to a new product is high-signal — it
  surfaces what the audience understood, what they didn't, and what they're excited
  about.
- Note any unexpected questions or misunderstandings. These are diagnostic — they point
  to gaps in the launch narrative or the channel assets.

### T+7

- First substantive post-launch content moment. Options:
  - User story or early case study, if strong use has emerged
  - Social proof aggregation (press coverage, notable mentions)
  - Founder or team perspective on what launch day revealed
- Internal debrief: what worked, what surprised us, what would we do differently?

### T+14

- Review product adoption signals in the context of the launch's channel performance.
  Which channels drove the most qualified adoption? What does that imply for the next
  launch?
- Identify the narrative thread for the next thirty days: what is the ongoing story
  of this product post-launch, and which channels carry it?

### T+30

- Full launch retrospective. Document: what the launch narrative was, what the channel
  coverage was, what the adoption signals looked like, and what we'd change. This
  becomes the input to the next product launch cadence.
- Identify whether any launch content should be updated, retired, or extended. Landing
  pages and product pages have a longer shelf life than announcement emails — are they
  still accurate and current?
