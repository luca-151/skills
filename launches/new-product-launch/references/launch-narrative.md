# Launch Narrative — How to Construct One

The launch narrative is the upstream strategic document for a new-product launch. It is
not a brief, not a messaging document, and not a tagline exercise. It is the connective
story that makes positioning executable across channels — the account of why this product
exists, why it exists now, and why we are the ones to have built it.

Every channel asset — email, social, landing page, press, long-form — is derived from
the launch narrative. If an asset cannot be traced back to the narrative, revise the
asset. If the gap is persistent, the narrative has a hole.

The narrative is written before asset production begins. It is typically two to four
pages. It is not published externally in its own form, though sections of it will appear
in almost verbatim form in long-form assets and press materials.

---

## What a launch narrative is not

It is not a product spec. The narrative is not a feature list with a story wrapped around
it. Features are evidence for the narrative's claims; they are not the narrative.

It is not a press release. A press release is a formal document structured for
journalists. The narrative is a strategic document structured for the launch team. It
will inform the press release; it is not the press release.

It is not a positioning statement. The positioning statement is upstream of the
narrative — it is one of the inputs. The narrative translates positioning into story.

It is not a pitch deck. A pitch deck is built for investors. The narrative is built for
an audience that does not yet know this product exists and needs to be made to care.

---

## The three-question frame

Every launch narrative answers three questions. They must be answered in specific,
falsifiable terms — answers that could be proven or disproven by evidence. Vague answers
produce vague launch communications, which produce vague audience responses.

### Why now

What has changed in the world — technically, culturally, behaviorally — that makes this
product newly necessary or newly possible?

The why-now is not a market-size argument ("the productivity software market is growing
at X%"). It is an account of a specific shift that has made the timing of this product
real. The best why-now arguments have three properties:

1. They point to a specific, observable change — not a trend, but an event or
   condition that the audience can verify from their own experience.
2. They imply that a window exists — that the moment is here but will not wait
   indefinitely. This is not manufactured urgency; it is an honest account of why
   this moment is the right one.
3. They connect to something the audience is already feeling. The why-now should
   feel like recognition, not instruction — the audience reads it and thinks "yes,
   that's exactly what's happening."

If the why-now is not clear, the product is either early or miscalibrated. An honest
why-now assessment is the most useful diagnostic for both.

### Why us

What does Every, and the specific team that built this product, know or have that makes
us the right builder?

Credibility claims here must be specific. General claims — "we're passionate about this
problem" — are not credibility. They are noise that every competitor can also claim.
Specific claims are anchored in observable evidence:

- Prior work in the domain (what have we built that demonstrates relevant competence?)
- Demonstrated insight (what do we understand about this problem that others don't, and
  how can we show we understand it?)
- Structural position (what access, relationships, or perspective does our position
  give us that others don't have?)
- Track record (what have we done before that is evidence of our judgment?)

The why-us does not require claiming superiority in all dimensions. It requires claiming
genuine credibility in the dimensions that matter most for this product.

### Why this

Why does this specific approach — these specific product decisions, this specific set of
capabilities and constraints — solve the problem better than alternatives?

The why-this answers the implicit question every audience member asks when they
encounter a new product: couldn't someone else build the same thing? The answer to that
question is no, and the why-this explains why. It points to the specific choices that
make this product what it is — including the things we chose not to do — and explains
the reasoning behind those choices.

The why-this is where product philosophy lives. It is the clearest statement of the
intellectual bet behind the product: we believe X about this problem, and that belief
led to these decisions. The audience should be able to disagree with the why-this.
If no one could disagree with it, it is not a specific enough claim.

## The founder / team credibility component

For new-product launches, the people behind the product are often as important as the
product itself. Who built it, why they built it, and what their relationship to the
problem is — these are credibility signals that channel assets frequently reference.

The launch narrative should include a brief account of:

- Who is most directly associated with building this product (name, role, relevant history)
- What their relationship to the problem is — did they experience it themselves? Have
  they studied it? Do they have a specific insight that led to this approach?
- What the credibility claim is — why should an audience trust that this team has
  the judgment to build this product well?

This component feeds directly into bylines, thought-leadership pieces, and press pitches.
It should be written in a form that is quotable without being written as a quote.

## Narrative arc across the launch

A new-product launch has three phases. The launch narrative should address how the story
develops across all three — not just what the launch-day message is.

### Pre-launch: building the tension

The pre-launch period establishes the problem and the moment without yet revealing the
product. The goal is to make the audience feel the tension the product will resolve —
to make them aware of the problem the product addresses, in a way that makes the product's
arrival feel satisfying rather than arbitrary.

Pre-launch content is not a teaser campaign. It is sincere engagement with the problem
space. What are the tensions the audience is living with? What is the conversation that
is happening in the audience's world right now, that this product enters? Pre-launch
content makes Every part of that conversation before the product is announced.

### Launch: the resolution moment

The launch day is the moment the product enters the conversation as the answer to the
tension the pre-launch period established. Every channel asset on launch day is a version
of the same story: here is the problem, here is the moment, here is what we built, here
is why we built it this way, here is how to get it.

The resolution should feel earned — not because of the marketing volume, but because
the pre-launch period established the problem and the launch narrative makes the product's
response to it feel specific and credible.

### Post-launch: proving the narrative

The post-launch period is where the narrative is validated or adjusted by evidence.
What are early users actually doing with the product? What is the feedback revealing
about whether the launch narrative was accurate? Are there user stories or outcomes that
confirm the why-now and why-this?

Post-launch content is not about keeping the buzz alive. It is about moving from
narrative to evidence — from "here is what this product will do" to "here is what this
product is actually doing." The two most valuable post-launch content types are user
stories (specific, named examples of the product in use) and narrative updates (honest
reflections on what the launch revealed about the product and the market).

## How the narrative anchors channel assets

Each channel asset should be derivable from the launch narrative by answering: what does
this channel most need, from the narrative, to do its job?

- **Landing page**: primarily the why-now and why-this — the problem and the product's
  specific approach. The landing page is where the audience who arrives with intent
  learns what this is and why they should try it.

- **Email to subscribers**: primarily the why-now and why-us — the moment and the
  credibility. Subscribers trust Every's editorial judgment; the email is invoking
  that trust on behalf of a new product. It should feel like a recommendation from a
  trusted source, not a product announcement.

- **Email to prospects**: primarily the why-now and the product's core promise. This
  audience has less relationship with Every; the case must be made more directly.

- **Social posts**: primarily a single element of the why-now or why-this, compressed.
  Social cannot carry the full argument; it carries one specific claim or image that
  invites the audience to want more.

- **Press materials**: the full argument in formal structure — the who, what, when, why-
  now, why-us, and why-this, with the product's specific claims made in quotable form.
  Press is the channel where the launch narrative is most directly exposed; it must
  be the most carefully written.

- **Long-form (byline, thought-leadership)**: the narrative's deepest treatment —
  the intellectual case for the why-now at length, or the product philosophy behind
  the why-this. Long-form assets are where the launch narrative gets to make its
  fullest argument.

If a channel asset is pulling in a direction the narrative does not support, the asset
has drifted. The narrative is the standard; the asset is revised to match it, not the
other way around.

## What a strong launch narrative looks like

A strong launch narrative:

- Can be read aloud in under ten minutes
- Makes three specific, falsifiable claims (the why-now, why-us, why-this)
- Would make a thoughtful journalist want to write about the product
- Would make someone who doesn't need the product understand why others do
- Could not have been written about a different product with minor substitutions
- Does not use the words "revolutionary," "game-changing," "reimagined," or
  "the future of" in any form

A weak launch narrative:

- Describes the product's features rather than the product's argument
- Makes claims that every competitor could also make
- Requires the audience to already understand the problem to find the why-now convincing
- Treats the team's enthusiasm as a substitute for specific credibility evidence
- Reads as if it was written after the assets rather than before them
