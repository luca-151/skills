# Semiotics & Behavioral Heuristics — Distinctive Assets and Mental Availability

The theoretical grounding for two concepts that appear frequently in brand equity work:
*distinctive assets* and *mental availability*. Both concepts have been popularized by
Byron Sharp's *How Brands Grow* (2010), but their intellectual foundations predate Sharp
significantly and locate the concepts in more rigorous traditions. We draw on those
foundations rather than on Sharp's popularization.

---

## Semiotics — the theory of distinctive assets

Distinctive brand assets are non-name signals that reliably trigger the brand in memory:
colors, shapes, typefaces, sonic signatures, spatial arrangements, narrative patterns. The
question of how such signals work — how a color or a sound can carry a brand identity —
is answered by semiotics.

### Peirce — types of signs

Charles Sanders Peirce's tripartite sign taxonomy (from his collected papers, 1931-1935)
is the most operationally useful framework for analyzing brand assets. Peirce distinguishes
three modes by which a sign relates to its object:

**Icon**: the sign resembles its object. A photograph of a product is an icon. A brand
that looks like the thing it represents (a leaf shape for an organic food brand) deploys
iconicity. Icons are immediately legible but offer little distinctiveness — resemblance
is available to any brand in the category.

**Index**: the sign is causally connected to its object — it points to it by virtue of
an actual relationship. Smoke indexes fire. A product shown in a kitchen indexes cooking.
For brands, indexical signs work by contiguity: the brand appears consistently in
specific situations until appearance in that situation becomes an index of brand presence.
Orange for a financial services brand (ING) works partly through indexicality — enough
consistent use of orange creates a causal link in memory.

**Symbol**: the sign relates to its object through convention — arbitrary assignment
reinforced by use. Language is primarily symbolic. Most mature brand assets are symbolic:
the Nike swoosh has no natural connection to athletics or achievement; the connection
exists entirely through convention built by repeated pairing with relevant associations.

The practical implication: truly distinctive brand assets are primarily symbolic. They
work not because they resemble the brand's category or literally point to a product
benefit, but because convention has been established through consistent deployment. This
takes time and cannot be shortcut. It also means that assets which feel arbitrary —
a specific shade of a color, an unexpected word, an unexplained form — can become highly
distinctive because their arbitrariness makes them harder to confuse with other brands.

### Barthes — myth and connotation

Roland Barthes's *Mythologies* (1957) and *Elements of Semiology* (1964) provide the
framework for how brands encode and carry cultural meaning beyond their literal content.

Barthes distinguishes between denotation (the first-order meaning: what is literally
present) and connotation (the second-order meaning: the cultural and historical
associations that attach to what is present). Advertising is primarily connotative
work: the product is the denotative content; the cultural associations encoded in the
communication are the connotative content.

Barthes's concept of *myth* (developed in *Mythologies*) names the specific operation
by which connotation is naturalized — presented as given rather than constructed. A brand
that successfully deploys myth makes its associations feel inevitable and natural rather
than arbitrary and manufactured. The Marlboro cowboy naturalized a set of associations
(independence, masculinity, American landscape) that are in no literal sense inherent in
a cigarette. The naturalization — the sense that a Marlboro *is* freedom — is the mythic
operation.

For Every's brands: the challenge is to build myth without letting it calcify. Barthes
also observed that myth is conservative — it freezes cultural meaning and presents it as
eternal truth. Badge brands in cultural categories must refresh their myths as cultural
reference shifts, or the myth becomes kitsch.

### de Saussure — the arbitrary sign

Ferdinand de Saussure's *Course in General Linguistics* (1916) established the foundation
of modern semiotics. The key contribution for brand work: the *arbitrariness of the sign*.
The signifier (sound-image or visual mark) has no natural relationship to the signified
(the concept). The relationship is conventional, established through use within a system.

The practical implication for brand assets: there is no inherently "premium" color, no
inherently "innovative" typeface, no inherently "trustworthy" shape. These associations
are established by the system — by the conventions that have built up through use within
specific cultural contexts. Designing a distinctive brand asset means understanding the
existing sign system (what associations have already been established within the category
and culture) and either working within it (accepting existing conventions) or against it
(deliberately violating conventions to create distance). The most distinctive brand assets
tend to violate category conventions — they are readable as brand precisely because they
are not readable as category.

## Behavioral Heuristics — the theory of mental availability

Mental availability — the probability that a brand comes to mind in relevant purchase or
consumption contexts — is grounded in cognitive psychology, not in the Ehrenberg-Bass
research program. The concepts Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman developed in their work
on heuristics and biases (from 1973 onward, collected in *Judgment Under Uncertainty*,
1982) provide the theoretical foundation.

### Tversky and Kahneman — availability heuristic

The *availability heuristic* (Tversky & Kahneman, 1973) describes how people estimate
the frequency or probability of an event by how easily examples come to mind. What is
easy to retrieve feels common; what is difficult to retrieve feels rare. This cognitive
operation is not a flaw — it is an efficient heuristic that works well in most conditions.

For brands: a brand that comes to mind easily in a relevant context benefits from the
availability heuristic. The consumer implicitly treats ease of recall as evidence of
trustworthiness, prevalence, or relevance. Building mental availability is the work of
making the brand easy to retrieve in specific contexts — which requires (a) building
strong memory structures linking the brand to those contexts, and (b) maintaining those
structures through consistent brand signals over time.

Availability is not uniform. Retrieval ease is context-dependent: a brand can be highly
available in one set of circumstances and absent in another. Brand-building work should
target the specific contexts — the category entry points — in which the brand needs to
fire, not availability in the abstract.

### Category entry points

The situations, occasions, and mental states that trigger category need. Category entry
points (CEPs) are the contexts in which a brand needs to be available to compete. A
brand that comes to mind in many relevant CEPs has high breadth of availability; a brand
that comes to mind very readily in one or two CEPs has high depth but narrow breadth.

For Every's Studio products that have clear purchase occasions (Cora as an email
management tool, for example), CEP mapping is a productive diagnostic: which situations
trigger the need this product addresses, and how strongly is the brand linked to those
situations in the target audience's mental model?

For Every's master brand and for products like Plus One (where the purchase occasion is
less categorical and more identity-driven), CEP analysis is less productive. The
triggering condition for a badge brand subscription is not a specific task-need — it is
an ambient identity orientation. Mental availability for these brands is built through
cultural presence rather than category-entry-point saturation.

## On Byron Sharp

Byron Sharp's *How Brands Grow* (2010) popularized both distinctive assets and mental
availability, and the popularization has been useful: it put these concepts into broad
marketing practice and provided a corrective to the over-emphasis on brand differentiation
that characterized the strategic planning tradition. Sharp's insistence that growth comes
from reaching light and non-buyers — and that reach matters more than depth of
relationship for most brands in most categories — is empirically well-supported for large
CPG brands competing in mature markets.

Where Sharp's framework does not generalize: badge brands, loyalty-as-identity brands,
and subscription software products. Sharp's predictions are derived from Dirichlet models
of purchasing behavior in CPG categories, where switching costs are low, loyalty is
behaviorally weak, and brand choice is heavily habitual. In these conditions, the
claim that brand loyalty is largely an artifact of market share (rather than a target of
brand building) is defensible. It is not defensible for brands where switching costs
include identity costs — where using the brand is an expression of who you are, and
where defection would require a revision of self-concept. Every's brands operate in this
space. Loyalty exists for our brands in ways that Sharp's framework cannot accommodate.
His distinctive assets concept and his salience emphasis are useful; his predictions about
loyalty, penetration economics, and the futility of differentiation are not applicable
to our context. We use the tools; we reject the extrapolations.
