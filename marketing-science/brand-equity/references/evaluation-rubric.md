# Evaluation Rubric — How We Score Brand Equity at Every

The operational rubric that translates brand equity theory into evaluable criteria for
Every's portfolio. The rubric outputs two parallel results for each evaluation:

- **Absolute score**: a numerical score per sub-component and per dimension (1–5 scale).
  Used to track movement over time.
- **Target-shape overlay**: the gap between the current absolute score and the brand's
  provisional target shape. Used to assess whether the brand is becoming what it should
  be, in addition to how strong it currently is.

The three scoring dimensions are not a hierarchy — they are parallel. A brand is not
evaluated sequentially; Identity Clarity, Cognitive Presence, and Cultural Position are
assessed simultaneously and read in relation to each other.

---

## Dimension 1 — Identity Clarity

**Primary sources**: Aaker (brand identity system), Kapferer (culture and self-image
facets). Full reference: `references/aaker.md`, `references/kapferer.md`.

Identity Clarity measures how well-defined, coherent, and internally consistent the
brand's identity system is. It is a supply-side dimension — what the brand *is*, not what
audiences hold.

### Sub-components

**1.1 Core identity definition** — Is the brand's timeless essence articulable without
reference to specific products or current category positioning? Does the core identity
survive a thought experiment in which the brand's products are replaced?

**1.2 Extended identity coherence** — Do the brand's texture elements (personality,
visual language, verbal register, associations) hang together without contradiction?
Extended identity may evolve; it should not contradict itself within a given moment.

**1.3 Brand essence compression** — Can the brand be expressed in a single organizing
thought — a phrase or sentence that makes the core intelligible and implies the
extended identity without exhausting it? This is not a tagline test; it is a coherence
test.

**1.4 Four-perspective coverage** — Does the brand have defined, consistent expressions
across Aaker's four perspectives: brand-as-product, brand-as-organization,
brand-as-person, brand-as-symbol? Score each perspective; note which are absent or
underdeveloped.

**1.5 Distinctive assets (semiotic grounding)** — Are the brand's non-name signals
(color, form, voice, rhythm, spatial arrangement) genuinely distinctive and grounded in
sign systems that produce consistent decoding? Distinctive assets are evaluated on two
criteria: (a) whether they are actually distinctive relative to category conventions,
and (b) whether the semiotic conventions they invoke are stable and coherent. Source:
Peirce, Barthes, de Saussure (see `references/semiotics-heuristics.md`), not Sharp.

**1.6 Culture facet (Kapferer)** — Does the brand embody a coherent cultural value
system that generates identity rather than merely reflects it? The culture facet is what
gives the brand the capacity to produce appropriate expressions in new contexts — the
immune system of the identity. A brand with no culture facet must be told what to do;
a brand with a strong culture facet knows.

**1.7 Self-image facet (Kapferer)** — Does the brand enable a consistent interior
narrative for the user about who they are when they use the brand? Is the self-image
specific enough to be meaningful and accurate enough to be believed?

### Scoring anchors (1–5)

| Score | Description |
|---|---|
| 5 | All sub-components defined and mutually reinforcing. Core is durable. Extended identity is coherent. All four perspectives populated. Distinctive assets are genuinely distinctive and semiotically stable. Culture facet is generative. Self-image is specific and credible. |
| 4 | Most sub-components defined and coherent. One or two gaps or tensions. Assets are recognizable. Culture facet present but not fully generative. |
| 3 | Core is defined but extended identity has visible tensions. Assets are inconsistent across executions. Culture facet is implicit rather than explicit. |
| 2 | Core identity is contested or underspecified. Assets conflict. Culture facet is absent or contradictory. |
| 1 | No coherent identity system. Brand is functionally a product name with no surrounding architecture. |

## Dimension 2 — Cognitive Presence

**Primary sources**: Keller CBBE pyramid, Tversky/Kahneman availability heuristic, CEPs
where applicable. Full reference: `references/keller.md`,
`references/semiotics-heuristics.md`.

Cognitive Presence measures the depth and structure of brand knowledge in the target
audience's mind. **This dimension is always scored relative to a specified target
audience.** The reference audience must be stated before scoring; a score without a
reference audience is meaningless.

### Sub-components

**2.1 Salience (audience-relative)** — Probability the brand comes to mind in relevant
contexts for the target audience. Depth (how readily) and breadth (in how many relevant
situations) are assessed separately. A24 measured among film enthusiasts, not general
moviegoers. Cora measured among professionals managing high inbox volume. Every master
measured among intellectually ambitious professionals with a serious interest in AI's
implications.

**2.2 Performance associations** — Strength and valence of product or service associations
in the target audience's mental model. Does the audience have clear, positive beliefs
about what the brand actually does and how well?

**2.3 Imagery associations** — Non-product associations: user imagery (what kind of person
uses this), usage situation imagery (where and how), brand heritage, and brand values as
held by the target audience. Imagery is where badge brand equity primarily lives.

**2.4 Judgments (quality/credibility/superiority)** — How does the target audience
evaluate the brand on quality and credibility dimensions? Is the brand perceived as
genuinely excellent, credible, and superior to alternatives — or is it merely known?

**2.5 Feelings** — What emotional responses does the brand evoke in the target audience?
Note the specific quality of feeling: warmth, excitement, security, social approval,
self-respect, intellectual stimulation. For badge brands, self-respect and social approval
are often primary.

**2.6 Resonance** — Evidence of Keller's four resonance dimensions in the target audience:
behavioral loyalty (repeat use, subscription renewal), attitudinal attachment (genuine
affection, brand defense), sense of community (identification with other users),
active engagement (sharing, recommending, attending, contributing).

**2.7 Category Entry Points (Studio products)** — For Studio products with clear purchase
occasions, which CEPs does the brand own in the target audience's memory, and how strong
is the linkage? This sub-component is not applicable for identity-driven subscriptions
where the purchase occasion is ambient rather than categorical.

### Scoring anchors (1–5)

| Score | Description |
|---|---|
| 5 | Brand is spontaneously recalled in relevant contexts; strong, positive, differentiated associations at all pyramid levels; evidence of resonance behaviors across all four dimensions. |
| 4 | Brand is recalled with prompt in most relevant contexts; most associations positive and differentiated; some resonance evidence (attitudinal attachment or community). |
| 3 | Brand is known but not strongly differentiated in associations. Recall is prompted, not spontaneous. Performance associations are present; imagery associations are weak. |
| 2 | Brand has awareness but associations are weak, neutral, or contradicted. No evidence of resonance. |
| 1 | Brand is unknown to target audience or has negative associations that override any positive equity. |

## Dimension 3 — Cultural Position

**Primary sources**: Holt (iconic strategy), Bourdieu (distinction), Hebdige
(recuperation), plus Agamben, McCracken, Goffman, W. David Marx. Full references:
`references/holt.md`, `references/cultural-capital.md`,
`references/cultural-practitioners.md`.

Cultural Position measures whether the brand occupies a genuine position in the cultural
field — whether it resolves a real tension, confers cultural capital, and maintains
distinctiveness from the mainstream. This is the dimension most specific to Every's
positioning context and the one most susceptible to deterioration through recuperation.

### Sub-components

**3.1 Myth legibility** — Is the brand's cultural myth legible to its target audience?
Can we articulate the tension the brand resolves? Does the audience recognize and
resonate with that articulation, or does it feel manufactured? Myth must engage a genuine
tension — one the audience lives with — or it reads as hollow.

**3.2 Cultural capital conferral** — Does the brand confer cultural capital on its users?
How much and of what type (embodied, objectified, symbolized)? Does use of the brand
signal meaningful cultural position to others in the relevant social field?

**3.3 Distinction clarity** — Is the brand clearly distinguished from mainstream culture
within its category? Is the distinction meaningful to the target audience — does it
represent genuine cultural specificity rather than mere stylistic difference?

**3.4 Subcultural authenticity** — Does the brand draw on genuine subcultural or
intellectual source material, or is it appropriating aesthetics without depth? Is the
cultural position grounded in real fluency and participation in the source worlds, or
in observed style?

**3.5 Identity value** — Does the brand serve as a meaningful prop in users'
self-presentation (Goffman)? Do users deploy the brand visibly as part of their identity
performance? Is the brand the kind of thing people mention, share, or display because
doing so says something about who they are?

**3.6 Recuperation Risk** — [Standing monitor. Separate from overall Cultural Position
score. Evaluated on a different cadence: quarterly, or when a canary signal fires.]

Recuperation risk is not a sub-component of the Cultural Position score — it is a
parallel standing monitor. It measures the rate at which the brand's cultural
distinctiveness is being absorbed by the mainstream. The score is qualitative:
**Low / Moderate / High / Critical**.

Named canary signals:
- Brand mentioned in mainstream tech press roundups without distinguishing framing
  (treated as a category representative rather than a distinctive voice)
- Brand appearing in YouTube comparison videos against generic competitors
- Brand's distinctive language, aesthetic, or editorial framing appearing in competitor
  marketing copy
- Brand cited approvingly by audiences explicitly outside the originally targeted
  audience, without evidence of the brand moving to address that audience intentionally

When any canary signal fires: escalate to Moderate. When two or more: escalate to High.
When the brand's own audience begins to read it as mainstream: Critical.

Recuperation risk is monitored quarterly and reported separately from the full evaluation.
The full evaluation captures a point-in-time equity snapshot; the recuperation monitor
is a trailing indicator of cultural position durability.

### Scoring anchors (1–5)

| Score | Description |
|---|---|
| 5 | Brand resolves a genuine cultural tension that the target audience recognizes. Strong cultural capital conferral in the relevant social field. Clearly distinct from mainstream. Source material is specific and fluent. High identity value; brand is deployed as a self-presentation prop. No recuperation risk. |
| 4 | Brand has a legible myth. Cultural capital conferral is recognized by target audience. Some mainstream adjacency but not absorbed. Identity value present. Recuperation risk low. |
| 3 | Brand has cultural signals but myth is underspecified. Cultural capital is claimed but not yet conferred by the target audience. Distinction is visible but not yet earned. |
| 2 | Brand is culturally ambiguous. Neither distinctively subcultural nor coherently mainstream. Minimal cultural capital conferral. |
| 1 | Brand has no cultural position or is actively culturally negative. |

## McLuhan Supplementary Diagnostic

Not scored. Applied after the three-dimension evaluation. See `references/mcluhan-media.md`
for full methodology. Three outputs:

1. **Brand temperature assessment**: hot, cool, or mixed
2. **Media temperature mapping**: the temperature of the brand's primary channels
3. **Temperature-medium fit check**: pass or mismatch; if mismatch, describe the failure
   mode and channel affected
4. **Tetrad**: enhances, obsolesces, retrieves, reverses

## Target-Shape Scoring

Every brand in the portfolio has a provisional target shape — a set of dimension targets
that reflects the strategic role the brand plays in the Every system. The rubric outputs
both the absolute score (how strong is this brand?) and the gap from target (is this
brand becoming what it should be?).

### Provisional target shapes

| Brand | Identity Clarity | Cognitive Presence | Cultural Position | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Every master | 5 | 4 | 5 | Badge brand; full expression of Every's thesis |
| Plus One | 4.5 | 3.5 | 4.5 | Badge brand; cultural position and identity clarity are primary objectives |
| Cora | 4.5 | 4.5 | 3.5 | Proof point brand; narrow CEP ownership plus disproportionate Cultural Position for its category. Not 2.5 — Cora is too visible a product and too central to Every's studio thesis to hold a low cultural target. |
| Spiral | TBD | TBD | TBD | Pending strategic positioning decisions |
| Monologue | TBD | TBD | TBD | Pending strategic positioning decisions |
| Sparkle | TBD | TBD | TBD | Pending strategic positioning decisions |
| Proof | TBD | TBD | TBD | Pending strategic positioning decisions |

**Strategic logic note**: Studio products that serve as visible proof points for Every
master's thesis carry higher Cultural Position targets than Studio products serving
primarily utility functions. Cora's position as a culturally significant product (used
and discussed by the same audiences who read and discuss Every's editorial output) justifies
a Cultural Position target above what a pure utility tool would carry.

### Gap assessment

For each dimension:
- **Green**: current absolute score is within 0.5 of target
- **Yellow**: current absolute score is 0.5–1.0 below target
- **Red**: current absolute score is more than 1.0 below target

A brand with a red gap in Cultural Position requires different work than a brand with a
red gap in Cognitive Presence. The dimension-specific rubric and the reference files
provide the diagnostic vocabulary for identifying what the gap means and what work
would close it.

## Tracking Cadence

**Full evaluation**: annually, or when a strategic decision triggers a reappraisal
(significant repositioning, major launch, competitive shift, evidence of substantial
brand perception change). A full evaluation is time-intensive; do not over-apply it.

**Recuperation risk monitor**: quarterly. Separate from the full evaluation. Checks
the named canary signals and updates the risk level. Takes less than thirty minutes with
current signal awareness. Does not require scoring all three dimensions.

**Off-cycle triggers** (conditions that warrant an unscheduled evaluation):
- A brand appearing in mainstream press roundups without distinguishing framing
- A competitor adopting the brand's vocabulary or aesthetic conventions
- A launch or campaign that may have materially shifted brand perception
- A strategic decision that changes the brand's target shape

---

## Scoring Inputs

The rubric is not purely qualitative, but most inputs are interpretive rather than
quantitative. Primary inputs:

- Qualitative audience interviews (see `marketing-science/research/references/discovery-interviews.md`)
- Competitive audit findings (what signals the category uses, and where our brands diverge)
- Press and media monitoring (for recuperation canary signals and identity clarity signals)
- Social monitoring (how audiences describe and deploy the brand in their own voice)
- Internal creative audit (whether brand executions are consistent with identity system)
- Periodic structured surveys (for awareness and association mapping in target segments)

Quantitative signals (audience-relative salience estimates, subscription renewal rates,
referral patterns) are useful as anchors but do not substitute for the qualitative
evaluation. Brand equity is built in meaning, and meaning resists reduction to metrics.
