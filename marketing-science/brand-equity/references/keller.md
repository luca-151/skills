# Keller — CBBE Pyramid & Brand Resonance

Kevin Lane Keller's Customer-Based Brand Equity (CBBE) model — the consumer-side lens
on how brand equity builds from initial awareness through to deep loyalty and resonance.
Where Aaker describes what a brand *is*, Keller describes what consumers *hold* — the
cognitive and emotional structure of brand knowledge in the audience's mind.

CBBE is always measured relative to a target audience, not the general population. A24
has profound resonance among cinephiles; measuring it against the general moviegoing
public produces a false deflation. Cora's salience is measured among professionals
managing high inbox volume, not office workers broadly. Every evaluation must specify
its reference audience or the scores are meaningless.

---

## CBBE Pyramid

The pyramid has four levels, each a prerequisite for the one above. Equity builds bottom
to top. Diagnose from the bottom up — if resonance is weak, check whether the failure
is at salience, meaning, or response first.

### Identity — "Who are you?" (Salience)

The foundation of the pyramid. Salience is the depth and breadth of brand awareness:
depth is how readily the brand comes to mind; breadth is the range of purchase and
consumption situations in which the brand comes to mind. Both matter. A brand can have
high depth but narrow breadth — it comes to mind very readily in one context but nowhere
else.

For badge brands, salience among the right audience is more valuable than salience
among a large audience. Breadth should expand within the target segment before expanding
across segments.

Salience is not the same as recognition or unaided recall, though those are proxies. True
salience is contextual — the brand fires when a relevant situation arises, not just when
someone is directly asked about the category. Building salience means building strong,
varied links between the brand and the contexts in which it should come to mind.

### Meaning — "What are you?" (Performance + Imagery)

The second level has two blocks, representing what Keller calls the *rational* and
*emotional* routes to brand meaning.

**Performance** — the functional associations: what does the brand do, and how well?
Includes product reliability, durability, service quality, design, price appropriateness,
and efficacy. Performance associations are the rational substrate on which the rest of
the brand structure rests. Without credible performance associations, the rest of the
pyramid lacks foundation.

For Every's software products, performance associations include: AI accuracy and
usefulness, interface quality, speed, stability, and whether the product actually helps
users do the thing they came to do.

**Imagery** — the non-product associations: who uses this brand, in what situations, and
what does the brand's history and values imply about its character? Imagery encompasses
user profile imagery (what kind of person uses this), usage situation imagery (where and
how is it used), brand history and heritage, and brand personality. Imagery associations
are what transform a product into a brand.

For Every's brands, imagery is where most identity work lives — the associations built
through editorial voice, visual language, writer profiles, and cultural positioning.

### Response — "What about you?" (Judgments + Feelings)

The third level captures how audiences *respond* to a brand — the evaluative and
emotional reactions that emerge from accumulated salience and meaning.

**Judgments** — the active evaluations audiences make: quality (how good is this?),
credibility (can I trust this?), consideration (would I use this?), and superiority (is
this better than alternatives?). Judgments are rational in form but often shaped by
associations rather than direct product experience.

**Feelings** — the emotional responses the brand evokes: warmth, fun, excitement,
security, social approval, self-respect. Feelings are not always overtly emotional; they
can be low-intensity affective colorings of the brand — the mild pleasure of using
something that feels well-made, or the quiet assurance of using something that signals
taste. For badge brands, social approval and self-respect are often the dominant feeling
dimensions.

### Relationships — "What about you and me?" (Resonance)

The summit of the pyramid. Resonance is the depth of the psychological bond between
brand and audience — the degree to which the brand has become a meaningful part of the
user's life and self-concept. Keller identifies four dimensions:

**Behavioral loyalty** — repeat purchase or use; the subscription renewal, the habitual
return. Necessary but not sufficient for resonance.

**Attitudinal attachment** — genuine affection for the brand, beyond preference.
Attitudinal attachment is what causes someone to defend a brand to a skeptic, to feel
mild distress when the brand disappoints, or to describe it as a favorite rather than just
a regular choice.

**Sense of community** — identification with other brand users; the feeling of shared
identity created by being part of the same audience. This is particularly powerful for
media brands, where the subscriber/reader identity is partly constituted by what one
chooses to read. Every has this asset — being an Every reader is a signal about taste and
intellectual orientation.

**Active engagement** — going beyond passive consumption: sharing content, contributing
to community, attending events, recommending to others. Active engagement is the hardest
dimension to build and the clearest indicator of deep resonance.

## Brand Resonance and Badge Brands

Resonance is where badge brands diverge from utility brands. For a utility brand (a
project management tool, a cloud storage service), resonance is primarily behavioral
loyalty — the switching cost is sufficient. For a badge brand, attitudinal attachment
and sense of community are the primary resonance targets. The brand should feel like
something worth belonging to, not just something that works.

Every's brands aspire to the resonance profile of media institutions more than software
utilities. The subscription to The New Yorker is not renewed primarily because the reader
calculates word count delivered per dollar — it is renewed because being a New Yorker
reader is part of who the subscriber understands themselves to be. That is the resonance
model we are building toward.

## When to use Keller at Every

Keller is the diagnostic instrument for audience-side equity. Load when:

- Evaluating where equity is failing in the pyramid (start from bottom, look for the
  broken rung)
- Assessing whether a brand has achieved resonance or is stuck at meaning/response
- Understanding whether salience is broad enough within the target segment
- Diagnosing whether imagery and performance associations are aligned or contradicting
- Distinguishing between audience segments — a brand can have different pyramid profiles
  for different audience cohorts

In the three-dimension scoring model, Keller is the primary source for Cognitive Presence
(dimension 2). Audience-relative scoring is mandatory: specify the reference audience
before running the diagnostic.

## Limitations and complements

Keller's model is strong on cognitive and emotional equity and weak on cultural equity.
The pyramid has no native vocabulary for the social tension a brand resolves, the cultural
capital it confers, or its relationship to subculture and recuperation. Holt, Bourdieu,
and Hebdige fill that gap. Keller also assumes that performance associations are the
foundation of meaning — for badge brands, this can invert: imagery sometimes precedes
performance in determining perceived quality. Load Holt alongside Keller for cultural
brands.
