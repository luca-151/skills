# Competitive Audits

How we audit competitors at Every — what we look at, how we structure findings, and how
audits feed into positioning and messaging. Codified July 2026 from the Cora 2.0, Plus
One/Every Agent, and All Access audit cycles. The operational version lives in the
competitive-audit launch skill; this file is the methodology it implements.

---

## Audit scope

Eight dimensions per competitor, checked live, every claim cited with a link and date:
messaging (hero line, pillars, the enemy they name), positioning (who it's for, the
axes they compete on), pricing (tiers, numbers, trial mechanics), visual identity
(type, color, motion — reference screenshots), tone of voice, proof (logos, stats,
testimonials and their sourcing), channels (where they actually show up), and naming
(product + feature naming conventions).

## Competitor selection

Three rings: direct (same job, same buyer), indirect (same job, different mechanism),
aspirational (whose brand position we'd want at scale). Name 3–5 direct, 2–3 indirect,
1–2 aspirational. The set is proposed, then ruled by the audit owner — it is a strategic
choice, not a search result.

## Audit methodology

The epistemic bar is fixed before the audit starts and does not move: every claim about
what a competitor says, charges, or does requires a live check cited inline; "I
recognize this brand" is unverified until a search confirms it; anything not checked is
named as unchecked in the deliverable. Check every competitor on every dimension the
same way — no sampling, no skipping the boring ones.

## Analysis framework

Two-axis positioning map, axes chosen per category (the Cora precedent: Agent vs. Tool ×
Crafted vs. Generic). Every competitor placed with the evidence for its placement.
Whitespace is a quadrant no one credibly occupies — and the analysis must say whether
it's empty because it's valuable or because it's worthless.

## Output format

One table (competitor × the eight dimensions, each cell cited), the positioning map,
a claims-we-cannot-make list (things competitors already own), and a whitespace verdict.
Verified facts visibly separate from interpretation throughout.

## Feeding into positioning

Positioning consumes three things from the audit: the whitespace verdict (where to
stand), the claims-we-cannot-make list (what to avoid), and the enemy analysis (what
the category's incumbents make customers feel, which is what our enemy framing attacks).
The gut-check (launches/codex-gut-check) re-consumes the audit at Week 5 to
pressure-test copy; audits older than ~4 weeks get their load-bearing claims re-verified.
